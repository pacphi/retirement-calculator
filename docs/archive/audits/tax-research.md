# Tax Research: Additional Effective Income-Tax Rate by Location

**Scope:** US-citizen couple drawing US-source retirement income (Washington DRS pension, traditional IRA/401(k) withdrawals, Social Security). US federal income tax is already modeled. This document determines the **additional** effective income-tax rate beyond federal, as a single modeled percentage, for each supported location.

**Income baseline used:** ~$100,000–$150,000 gross retirement income (pension + IRA; Social Security modeled separately; couple filing jointly where applicable).

**Research date:** June 2026.

## 2. Treaty & FTC Mechanics

A US citizen abroad owes US federal income tax on worldwide income regardless of where they live — this is a foundational rule of the US tax system. When that citizen also becomes a tax resident of a foreign country, the foreign country may also claim the right to tax the same income under its domestic law. To prevent double taxation, two main mechanisms apply:

**Foreign Tax Credit (Form 1116):** The US allows a dollar-for-dollar credit against US federal tax for income taxes paid to foreign countries on the same income. This credit is limited to the US tax attributable to foreign-source income (the FTC limitation). For US-source retirement income (pensions, IRA distributions), the FTC mechanics are more complex: if a foreign country taxes US-source pension income, the US citizen can generally claim the FTC, but only to the extent the US itself would tax that income.

**The "pay the higher, not both" principle:** In practice, if the foreign effective tax rate on retirement income is *lower* than the US effective rate, the FTC wipes out the US tax on that portion and the total tax paid equals approximately the *foreign* rate. If the foreign rate is *higher* than the US rate, the FTC absorbs US federal entirely, but the excess foreign tax above the US liability is an additional, generally non-recoverable burden. This is why France (exclusive US taxing rights under treaty) and Italy/Greece (7% flat < US effective) show $0 additional burden, while Austria and the Netherlands (rates ~38–40%+ effective) show meaningful additional burden.

**Treaty assignment of taxing rights:** Many treaties assign primary taxing rights over private pensions to the country of residence (common rule) — meaning the foreign country taxes the pension and the US can apply the FTC. Some treaties, notably the US-France treaty Article 18, give *exclusive* taxing rights to the source country (US) over US-source pensions, meaning France cannot tax them at all. These treaty-specific assignments materially affect the net burden.

**Savings clause:** Nearly all US tax treaties include a savings clause allowing the US to tax its citizens as if the treaty did not exist. This limits how much treaty benefits reduce US tax for US citizens, but the FTC remains fully available regardless of the savings clause.

**Locations genuinely higher:** The Netherlands (Box 1 ~37–40% effective on pensions) and Austria (~38–40% effective) create meaningful additional burden for US citizen retirees because their effective rates substantially exceed the typical US federal effective rate at retirement income levels. Spain also generates meaningful additional burden under the standard IRPF regime. Portugal post-NHR is materially higher but remains low-confidence given regime transition.

## 4. Method & Caveats

**Effective vs. marginal rates:** All figures in this table represent estimated *effective* rates — the total additional tax as a fraction of gross retirement income — not the marginal rate on the last dollar. Effective rates are substantially lower than top marginal rates for progressive-bracket systems.

**Single-rate simplification:** The calculator requires one number per location. In practice, the additional rate depends on income composition (more pension vs. more IRA vs. more Social Security), filing status, applicable deductions, the resident country's treatment of specific US vehicle types (IRA, 401(k), DRS pension), and year-to-year changes in both US and foreign law. The rates in this table are reasonable representative estimates for a US couple with ~$100,000–$150,000 of total retirement income in 2026.

**Rapidly changing regimes:** Portugal's NHR-to-IFICI transition (2024–2025) and Italy's 7% regime expansion (April 2026) illustrate how quickly international retirement tax regimes change. Rates marked *low-confidence* should be revisited annually.

**Social charges and VAT:** Foreign social insurance charges (e.g., France's CSG/CRDS, Netherlands' national insurance contributions bundled into Box 1 rates) may or may not be creditable against US federal tax. This table focuses on income taxes only.

**Professional advice required:** Real cross-border retirement taxation is highly fact-specific. Treaty benefits, the FTC limitation calculation, state domicile questions, FBAR/FATCA reporting, and currency effects all affect the actual result. This table is a planning-grade approximation for a retirement projection tool, not tax advice. Households considering relocation should consult a qualified cross-border tax professional (e.g., a US CPA with international expertise or an enrolled agent familiar with the target country's treaty position) before making decisions.
